Gratitude on the Brain

Gratitude, though an emotion, is distinct from most other emotions in that it requires the acknowledgement of a benefactor. It isnâ€™t just a positive emotional response, but instead is associated with pure altruism, or the idea that it cannot be felt for social benefit or elevation of social status. It is purely recognizing that another person has done something on your behalf as a moral exemplar. Because gratitude is a complex emotion, much has been done in the way of psychological and sociological research, but little in the way of neurological studies. This page seeks to combine the results of four studies done on gratitude in the brain, in particular, analyzing fMRI scans from these studies. Using gratitude journaling, and other methods to solicit gratitude from participants, several regions of interest (ROIs) were identified. Below is a visual representation of these findings.

Neural gratitude research studies primarily employ simulations with reflection, or gratitude journaling to determine which ROIs are used for gratitude. Results from these studies show that journaling gratitude on a regular basis not only activates the ROIs above, but can also cause neural plasticity, leaving a person more grateful and altruistic over time. No matter how simple or complex, reflecting on gratefulness is good for us.
