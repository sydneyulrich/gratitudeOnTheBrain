Examining the Studies

For Gratitude on the Brain, four primary studies were used in tandem to better understand how gratitude works in the brain. All four studies utilized fMRIs, and either gratitude journaling or scenarios to illuminate regions of interest (ROIs). Here I will break down each study to better understand what these ROIs mean within the context of the study.

Study 1: Neural Correlates of Gratitude

Study 1 was performed over the course of three weeks with both a gratitude journal group and an active-neutral journal control group. Both groups were given fMRIs to determine which ROIs, if any, were affected by the practice of gratitude and their journaling efforts. Results from the study showed there to be an increase in the neural pure altruism response in the Ventromedial Prefrontal Cortex (vmPFC), as well as a response in the Nucleus Accumbens. These researchers were particularly interested in the relationship between gratitude and pure altruism, so their studies also focused on the extent to which this was the case. They found that levels of gratitude corresponded with neural pure altruism, and that gratitude biases the brain’s reward system toward rewards for others versus oneself (Fox et al., 2015). The vmPFC, the part of the brain that is associated with risk, fear, morality and decision making, resulted in increased neural measures of pure altruism after just three weeks of gratitude journaling, when compared to control group. In short, the study showed that practicing gratitude not only leaves us feeling better, but leads to more altruistic ventures, or a genuine interest and concern for others unrelated to oneself.

Study 2: The Effects of Gratitude Expression on Neural Activity

Working with subjects with depression and/or anxiety, study 2 used gratitude letter writing for three months, compared with a neutral letter writing group. After three months, they performed fMRI scans to compare gratitude in the brain. Results from the study showed greater and longer lasting neural sensitivity to gratitude, which, like study 1, also presented in the vmPFC. Researchers hypothesized that other ROIs should include the limbic system: anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC), the Anterior Insula, and the striatum, since these are associated with affect. Their studies were consistent with this thought, and activity was noted in the ACC, as well as the vmPFC, and Anterior Insula. Most interestingly though, the researchers saw lasting effects in the ACC in response to the gratitude journaling and suggest that this may suggest a neural plasticity effect. This is the idea that we can change our neurons in response to a stimulus, in this case, gratitude. The sagittal view, above, shows the vmPFC as well as the ACC. Not shown are the Anterior Insula, and the striatum, which also were shown as ROIs in this study.

Study 3: Neural Substrates and Social Consequences of Interpersonal Gratitude: Intention Matters

Researchers in study 3 hypothesized that gratitude would be associated with regions in the brain known to be used in moral cognition, value judgement and theory of mind. Simulating an experience in which the participants would either receive or not receive help from others, they measured, again, with an fMRI scan, gratitude in the brain. Their findings supported this hypothesis, showing brain activity in the ACC, vmPFC, and Posterior Cingulate Cortex (PCC). Moreover, after asking participants to reflect on the others’ choices to either help or not help each other, researchers noted activity in the thalamus, among other parts. Deactivated regions included the ACC and the PCC. They summarized that the feeling of gratitude, as a response to another person doing something for your benefit, may not only feel good, but also reduce pain, improve relationships and enhance the idea of paying it forward, suggesting that our cultural expectation to say thank you, or be grateful for what you have, may have bigger impact on our lives than simply being kind.
